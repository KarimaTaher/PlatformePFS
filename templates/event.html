Major Oil Prices

Brent Crude

Brent is often considered a more accurate indicator of global oil market conditions. It is extracted from the North Sea and is used mainly in Europe, Africa, and the Middle East.

It serves as a benchmark for nearly two-thirds of the world's oil contracts, with prices set in London markets. Due to its strategic importance and global recognition, Brent prices are sensitive to geopolitical eventsâ€”such as tensions in the Middle Eastâ€”and to OPEC decisions and global demand fluctuations.

On a daily basis, specialized consulting firms like SPI AM, Energi Danmark, DNB, and ActivTrades, among others, provide guidance to investors and banks, who also rely on their in-house analysts. Economic news agencies frequently report on these trends due to their broad impact on the global economy.

West Texas Intermediate (WTI)

WTI is a major reference for U.S. crude oil prices. It is primarily extracted from Texas oil fields and is known for its high quality and low sulfur content, making it a light and sweet crude.

WTI prices are determined at the Cushing oil hub in Oklahoma and are commonly used as a benchmark in the North American market. These prices are heavily influenced by domestic production levels, inventory status, and refinery demand in the U.S.

Technological advancements like shale oil extraction have significantly boosted WTI supply, transforming the United States into a major player in the global oil market.

History of Oil Price

The Brent market experienced a major peak in 2008, reaching around $150 before plummeting to $40 following the global financial crisis. Between 2010 and 2014, prices hovered near $100 due to a global economic recovery, but dropped in 2015 to about $30, largely due to oversupply and increased competition from shale producers.

In 2020, the COVID-19 pandemic caused Brent prices to crash to around $20. Since then, prices have rebounded, peaking at approximately $120 in 2022 due to post-pandemic recovery and supply disruptions triggered by geopolitical conflicts such as the war in Ukraine and issues in the Red Sea.

WTI also hit a historic high of about $150 in 2008, driven by strong demand and geopolitical tensions, only to crash to $40 later that year due to the financial crisis. Between 2010 and 2014, prices recovered and stabilized around $100, but declined again in 2015 to approximately $40 due to the U.S. shale boom.

In April 2020, WTI prices made history by dropping into negative territory as the pandemic slashed global demand. Since then, prices have surged again, reaching over $120 in 2022 due to economic recovery and geopolitical strains.

Why is there a price gap between Brent and WTI?

The price difference between Brent and WTI is mainly due to logistics and transportation constraints. Brent is more easily transported globally, while WTI has higher export costs.

In the U.S., oil transportation between domestic regions must be done using ships flying the American flag, crewed and repaired by Americans, using American-made partsâ€”making internal movement more expensive than exporting Brent.

Supply and Demand

Oil supply and demand directly influence prices in global markets: an increase in supply, such as overproduction by oil-producing countries, tends to lower prices, while rising demand typically pushes them up.

Production

Like any market, when production drops, prices tend to rise. A surplus of oil, such as during the U.S. shale oil boom (light tight oil), caused a significant drop in prices.

Some countries experience a collapse in production due to poor management, such as Venezuela. However, Venezuela's output has been recovering, with 2023 volumes rising by a third compared to 2020.

In general, it's common for some countries to fail to meet their production commitments, while others produce more than they declare.

Other countries have recently discovered oil reserves and started producing, thus increasing global supply. For instance, Guyana now produces over 600,000 barrels daily, up from virtually zero five years ago.

Consumption

Global oil consumption is around 100 million barrels per day (Mb/d). While demand keeps growing each year, it is expected to slow down with the electrification of transport.

Global demand is typically very dependent on the economy and climate. Still, oil demand is more stable than electricity demand and rarely declines.

Note that there's usually a wave of purchases on the first day of each month.

Stockpile Management

Weekly crude, gasoline, and distillate stock levels are closely monitored by analystsâ€”especially the U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA) report and data from the American Petroleum Institute (API).

Rising stock levels tend to put downward pressure on prices, as seen in the week ending March 15, which reflects a normal seasonal dip.

During refinery maintenance periods, crude oil is typically stored in tanks.

Pricing Policies

Producing countries can choose to raise or lower their prices, especially to offload excess supply or when they urgently need cash. These direct deliveries influence overall supply and demand dynamics.
